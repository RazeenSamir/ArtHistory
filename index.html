A Period of Light,
Reason, and Form

Between 1650 and 1775, Europe moved from the years before the Enlightenment into the height of the period. Enlightenment thought brought new attention to reason, individual liberty, freedom of expression, and the relationship between religion and government.

This timeline makes it possible to compare how sculpture changed before and during the Enlightenment, especially through object type and material.

Across the Louvre collection, these objects and materials reveal a period shaped by tradition, experimentation, global exchange, and changing ideas about art's place in everyday life.

Research Question

How did object types
and materials shift
before and throughout
the Age of Enlightenment?

As Europe moved from the Baroque period into the Age of Enlightenment, the Louvre's collection shows changes in both object type and material. The dataset points toward more secular, decorative, and smaller sculptural forms. Materials shifted too: marble remained dominant, while terracotta and porcelain became more visible in works tied to domestic life, global exchange, and artistic experimentation.

The Language
of Materials

Every material was a choice — a statement about permanence, luxury, and cultural value. The Enlightenment had its own material vocabulary.

Marble

303 50.5%

A polished stone commonly used for formal sculpture. Its high frequency shows that classical, prestigious materials remained important throughout this period.

Bronze

84 14.0%

A cast metal used for durable sculpture, medals, and commemorative objects. Its lower presence suggests less focus on heavy public monuments compared to smaller object types.

Terracotta

79 13.2%

Fired clay that could be shaped more easily by hand. It connects to smaller works, studies, and a growing interest in movement, anatomy, and expression.

Porcelain

26 4.3%

A smooth ceramic material often linked to luxury and trade. Its presence reflects the period’s interest in decorative objects, global exchange, and domestic spaces.

Wood

25 4.2%

Used in furniture, frames, religious objects, and smaller decorative works. It shows that the collection includes interior and everyday objects, not just formal sculpture.

Other

83 13.8%

Includes less common materials such as wax, plaster, ivory, silver, stone, and mixed media. This category shows the wider material variety present in the Louvre dataset.

— Methodology —

The Data Collection
Process

Behind every exhibit lies a rigorous research process. Here is how raw museum metadata became a structured dataset focused specifically on sculpture and ready for analysis.

⟨/⟩
01

Web Extraction

The Louvre's sculpture collection returned around 40,000 results, which was too large for the scope of this project. The search was narrowed to works dated 1650–1775, covering the years before the Enlightenment through the height of the period.

⊞
02

Dataset Construction

That date range narrowed the collection to 724 results. Custom scripts then extracted titles, dates, materials, object types, image references, and collection information into a structured spreadsheet.

◈
03

Cleaning & Focus

Because our research focused specifically on sculpture, the dataset was cleaned around the two variables most relevant to our question: object type and material. The five object types came from Louvre classifications, and the final sample was filtered by metadata quality rather than hand-chosen. Entries with missing, unclear, or inconsistent information were removed or standardized, leaving 468 objects.

◉
04

Exploratory Visualization

Initial charts were generated to map distributions and temporal trends — transforming raw counts into interpretable patterns. This exploratory phase shaped which findings were worth pursuing further.

✦
05

Validation & Cross-Check

Results were cross-validated through three independent tools: Tableau (with guidance from a research librarian), Microsoft Excel, and Claude Opus 4.7. The AI-generated graphs diverged slightly from the others; Tableau and Excel were in agreement. The Excel outputs were selected as the definitive visualizations for this presentation.

Why this mattered: Opus likely diverged because it made interpretive choices around uncertain dates, decade boundaries, multi-material objects, and similar object-type labels. Excel and Tableau followed the cleaned spreadsheet categories more literally, so their matching results made Excel the most reliable source for the final charts.

— Case Studies —

Objects in Context

Two works from the collection illuminate the broader material and formal shifts visible in the data.

Marble · Statue · 1653–57
01

Cénotaphe du coeur du cardinal Pierre de Bérulle

Jacques Sarazin · 1653–57

Marble Statue 1650–1660

Jacques Sarazin's Cénotaphe du coeur du cardinal Pierre de Bérulle serves as an early baseline, showing the object and material pattern before the later eighteenth-century shift becomes more visible. Sarazin was an established French sculptor whose work often belonged to formal religious and elite settings — fitting the commemorative nature of this piece. As a marble statue, it reflects how important sculptural objects were still strongly tied to religion, authority, permanence, and public memory. The marble gives the piece cultural weight, making the memorial feel official and lasting rather than decorative or intimate. Sarazin's work anchors the earlier side of the trend, especially when contrasted with later pieces that move toward terracotta, porcelain, and smaller sculptural forms.

Note: Since our dataset begins at 1650, this piece is treated as the early baseline rather than a true pre-transition example — it still shows the older marble/statue pattern before the later shift becomes clearer. This represents a pattern, not an obsolete practice.
Terracotta · Sculpted Group · 1750
02

Anacréon arrachant une plume des ailes de l'amour

Augustin Pajou · 1750

Terracotta Sculpted Group 1750–1760

As the Louvre dataset moves into the Age of Enlightenment, many artists turned from religious iconography toward mythological figures rendered with softer textures and more intimate compositions. Augustin Pajou's Anacréon arrachant une plume des ailes de l'amour is a significant example of this shift in the 1750s — its delicate poses and warm surface convey themes of beauty, love, leisure, and elegance that defined the era's artistic sensibility. Pajou's choice of a mythological subject rather than a devotional one reflects the Enlightenment's secular turn. Our dataset records a resurgence of terracotta in the 1760s–1770s, and this work illustrates how artists embraced the material for its warmth, intimacy, and suitability for expressive small-scale sculpture.

Note: Although our dataset shows a marble resurgence in the 1750s, Pajou's use of terracotta here demonstrates how individual works can sit at the leading edge of a broader material shift — one that becomes statistically visible only in the following decades.

Key Findings

01

Marble Dominated

303 of 600 material instances — 50.5% — were marble, peaking at 60 in 1700–1710. It embodied Enlightenment ideals of reason, clarity, and classical precedent.

02

Sculpture Prevailed

Statuettes (136), sculptures (117), and busts (91) together account for 73.5% of the collection — reflecting the era's deep investment in figural representation.

03

Bronze & the 1680s

Bronze surged to 40 objects in 1680–1690 — its highest point — coinciding with Louis XIV's height of power and the political commissioning of commemorative works.

04

Terracotta's Late Rise

Terracotta peaked at 21 objects in 1760–1770, signaling a late-Enlightenment turn toward intimate portraiture, personal expression, and experimental form.
